# PLATFORMS.md — X vs. LinkedIn Playbooks

> The operator's voice stays constant. Format and pacing adapt.
>
> **Load only the section(s) that match `enabled_platforms` in `PROFILE.md`.** If the operator enabled just X, ignore the LinkedIn playbook (and vice versa). The "When the Same Idea Goes to Both" and "Cross-Platform Don'ts" sections apply **only when `enabled_platforms` is `both`.**

## X (Twitter)

### Format Fundamentals
- **Single post:** 1–280 chars (longer available on Premium, but brevity still wins).
- **Thread:** 3+ tweets linked. Use only when the idea is genuinely worth unrolling — never pad.
- **Quote tweet:** Adds your take to someone else's post. Low effort, high leverage when done well.
- **Reply:** Where 50%+ of growth happens for most accounts. Treat replies as content.

### What Performs
- First line is everything. If tweet 1 doesn't hook, the thread dies.
- Specific > abstract. "I spent $2,400 on a course that taught me one thing" beats "lessons from a bad purchase."
- Contrarian takes on things the operator actually has expertise in.
- Screenshots of DMs, code, notes (when non-confidential) outperform plain text.
- Lists in a single tweet ("3 things I wish I knew at 25:") land well — but only if the items are real and specific.

### Hook Patterns That Work (study, don't copy)
- Concrete number + unexpected outcome: "I shipped 47 features last year. Two of them made 90% of the revenue."
- Strong claim then evidence: "Most [X] advice is wrong. Here's what actually works."
- Observation + question: "Nobody I know under 30 uses [X]. What does that tell us?"
- Confession: "I've been wrong about [X] for years. Here's what changed my mind."

### Do NOT
- "🧵👇" emoji hook
- "This 👇" as standalone
- Thread-bombing every idea (single posts are underrated)
- Subtweets without context
- "Tag someone who needs this" at the end
- Manufactured "just shipped" announcements for things that aren't actually shipped
- Quote-tweeting to dunk on strangers

### Engagement Draft Rules
- Reply drafts: always feel like a response, not a pitch.
- Don't reply just to be seen. If the operator doesn't have something genuine to add, scroll past.
- When replying to a bigger account, bring new information or a specific question — not agreement.

### Thread Construction
1. **Tweet 1:** The hook. Can stand alone if someone doesn't read further.
2. **Tweet 2:** The premise. Why this matters.
3. **Tweets 3-N:** One idea per tweet. No run-on thoughts split across two tweets.
4. **Last tweet:** Land it. Don't tack on a CTA unless it's natural. "Follow for more" hurts more than it helps.

Thread length sweet spot: 5–9 tweets. 3 feels thin, 12+ feels like a blog post and should probably be one.

## LinkedIn

### Format Fundamentals
- **Post limit:** 3000 chars, but the sweet spot is 1200–1500.
- **The "see more" break:** The first 2–3 lines are visible before the click. Everything depends on whether those earn the click.
- **Native content wins:** Don't post links to your blog in the post body — put them in the first comment. LinkedIn's algorithm penalizes outbound links in posts.
- **Carousels (PDF documents):** High reach currently. Works when the content is genuinely visual-sequential.
- **Native video:** Outperforms text posts when the operator is comfortable on camera.

### What Performs
- Story format: situation → complication → lesson. Works when the story is real.
- Specific numbers grounded in specific projects.
- Taking a position that's mildly contrarian within the professional norms of the operator's field.
- Showing work (process, mistakes, learning) > showing results (trophies, metrics).
- Comments drive reach. The first hour of comments matters most — engage actively.

### Hook Patterns That Work
- **The counterintuitive claim:** "The best hire I ever made had no experience in the role."
- **The specific moment:** "Last Tuesday at 3pm, I realized my entire pricing strategy was backwards."
- **The confession:** "I've been running the wrong playbook for two years. Here's what I'm changing."
- **The observation:** "Every founder I talk to is making the same mistake right now."

### Do NOT (the "broetry" trap)
LinkedIn has a notorious style of one-line-per-sentence "broetry":

> I had a meeting.
>
> It didn't go well.
>
> Here's what I learned.
>
> 👇

This works on the algorithm. It also makes people roll their eyes. Use sparingly and only if it's genuinely how the operator sounds. Default to normal paragraphs.

### Hard Avoidances
- "Agree? 👇"
- "Double-tap if you agree"
- Humblebrags disguised as lessons ("Just closed our Series A — here's what I learned about resilience")
- Making every post about leadership even when it isn't about leadership
- Fake vulnerability ("I'm going to share something I've never shared before...")
- Posts that are 80% credentials and 20% actual content
- Using "I" in every single sentence of a post

### Professional Context Matters
LinkedIn is a semi-public record. Posts here are seen by:
- Current coworkers
- Former coworkers
- Prospective employers or clients
- Family members who somehow have LinkedIn accounts

When drafting, always ask: would the operator be comfortable if their CEO, their mom, and a potential customer all saw this? If any of them would wince, raise the concern.

### Comment Strategy
Commenting on bigger accounts in the operator's field is often higher ROI than posting. Draft rules:
- Add new information or a specific question. Never just "Great post!"
- Under 3 sentences is ideal.
- Don't pitch. Ever.
- If the operator has a relevant experience, share the 1-sentence version.

## When the Same Idea Goes to Both

> Applies only when `enabled_platforms` is `both`. Skip this section for single-platform operators.

Sometimes an idea works for both X and LinkedIn. Don't post identical text. Adapt:

- **X version:** Tighter. Funnier. More willing to be provocative. 1–3 tweets max.
- **LinkedIn version:** Fuller context. More professional framing. More story, less punchline.

Post them 12–24 hours apart, not simultaneously. Different audiences, but enough people follow on both that simultaneous cross-posts feel lazy.

## Cross-Platform Don'ts
- ❌ Copying an X thread into LinkedIn with tweet numbers still attached
- ❌ Copying a LinkedIn post onto X (the pacing will feel off)
- ❌ "I just posted this on LinkedIn →" (X users don't care)
- ❌ "Also posted on X →" on LinkedIn (same, reversed)

## Platform Analytics Focus

### X
Track: impressions, engagements, follower-quality (not just count), reply quality
Ignore: likes as a quality signal (they're the weakest metric now)

### LinkedIn
Track: impressions, dwell time, comments, profile visits, follows from posts
Ignore: reactions other than "insightful" and "celebrate" (the others are near-meaningless)
